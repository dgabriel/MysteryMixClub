## Overview

These personas are grounded in real people from a real music league. They are not archetypes invented to justify features — they are the humans this product is being built for. All personas are gender neutral; the community has an equal mix.

---

## Persona 1: The Organizer

**Who they are**

A musician and former competitive slam poet with deep roots in music community. Spent formative years playing in bands, has worked closely with working musicians, and has spent decades building and sustaining creative communities. Runs the league as an act of community stewardship — not just for fun, but because keeping people connected through music is a core life value.

**Relationship with music**

Music is identity. Sharing and discovering music with people they respect matters as much as the music itself. The league is infrastructure for a community they've been building for decades.

**Relationship with scoring**

Loves it. Gets a little defensive when others push back on it. But has watched people disengage because of scoring pressure, and genuinely understands why. Will champion inclusive features because they've seen the cost of not having them.

**Relationship with technology**

Comfortable — more than most in the group. Can handle configuration and setup without friction.

**Key behavior**

Crowd-sources round themes from the group. Shared ownership of the experience is a deliberate choice, not laziness. The league belongs to everyone.

**What they need from MysteryMixClub**

- Easy league creation and management
- Tools to propose and collect round theme suggestions from the group
- The ability to hold competitive and non-competitive players in the same league without friction
- Confidence that no one will fall off because the product didn't make room for them

**The tension they hold**

They are the person who has to simultaneously protect the scoring layer for players who love it and create space for players who don't. The product needs to make that easy.

## Persona 2: The Wise Elder

**Who they are**

A musician and poet, a little older than most in the group. Has spent a lifetime spanning genres — jazz to punk to hip hop to folk — and carries that breadth as a quiet point of pride. Not showy about it, just deeply confident.

**Relationship with music**

Music knowledge is identity. The ability to reach across genres and find the right song for the right moment is something they've cultivated over decades.

**Relationship with scoring**

Scoring is validation. A high score confirms they read the room *and* brought something worthy. It's not about beating others — it's about being seen as the person who knows music. Would be genuinely hurt if scoring disappeared.

**Relationship with technology**

Functional. Wants things to work without having to think about them.

**What they need from MysteryMixClub**

- A scoring system that feels meaningful, not arbitrary
- Round history and results they can look back on
- A platform that treats music submission as a considered act, not a casual gesture

**The tension they hold**

Scoring is deeply tied to their sense of self in this community. They're not playing against anyone — they're playing against their own standard.

---

## Persona 3: The Punk Iconoclast

**Who they are**

A poet with deep punk roots who still carries that ethos. Participating fully is the point. Has an inverted relationship with the leaderboard — coming in last is occasionally a badge of honor, proof of an uncompromising refusal to chase the group's consensus.

**Relationship with music**

Eclectic, principled, and occasionally deliberately contrary. Doesn't submit to win — submits to say something. Occasionally, unexpectedly, totally aligned with the group and genuinely delighted by it.

**Relationship with scoring**

Needs the leaderboard to exist so they can gleefully ignore it — or gleefully come in last. The score is almost beside the point, except as proof that they didn't sell out. Paradoxically, removing scoring would actually diminish their experience.

**Relationship with technology**

Gets by. Prefers simplicity.

**What they need from MysteryMixClub**

- A scoring system robust enough to be worth subverting
- No friction in the submission process — just get the song in
- A platform that doesn't take itself too seriously

**The tension they hold**

Their participation is performative in the best sense — they're playing a character the community loves. The product should never accidentally flatten that.

## Persona 4: The Vibe Player

**Who they are**

Not one person but a cluster of real players, each with a distinct reason for not wanting to participate in scoring. What they share: a genuine love of music, a desire to contribute to the community, and a need for the product to make space for them without making them feel like second-class participants.

**The Wounded**

Submitted a song they loved deeply. Got no points. Felt unseen in a moment of vulnerability. Scoring turned an act of sharing into a small rejection. Needs a way to contribute without putting their heart on the line every round.

**The Outsider**

Taste consistently didn't align with the group's. Scoring amplified that gap until participating felt punishing rather than joyful. *Already left the league.* Just Vibing is the feature that brings them back. This is the product's origin story.

**The Pure Contributor**

Never votes. Not a protest — just a personality. Wants to put music into the world and let it land however it lands. Has no interest in judging anyone else's submission and never has.

**The Situational Vibe Player**

Loves scoring. Finds it fun. Plays fully in most rounds. But hit a grief round and couldn't cast a vote — it felt like a violation of the moment. Needed a way to honor that round without opting out of the league entirely. Just Vibing needs to be available per-round, not just as a permanent setting.

**What all Vibe Players need from MysteryMixClub**

- Full participation in submission — their songs are equal and vital
- Visible but warmly framed status — "Just Vibing" not "opted out"
- No votes cast or received, but written notes welcomed and encouraged from others
- Eligibility for Most Noted — a way to be recognized that has nothing to do with scoring
- Per-round flexibility for situational vibe moments
- Zero friction to return to full scoring mode when they're ready

**The tension they hold**

They need to feel fully present in the league, not accommodated by it. The language and design around Just Vibing must never feel like a consolation prize.

## Cross-Persona Insights

**Scoring means different things to different people**
- For the Wise Elder: validation of knowledge and taste
- For the Punk Iconoclast: something to subvert
- For the Organizer: fun, with an asterisk
- For the Vibe Player: anything from irrelevant to actively harmful

**The product must hold all of these simultaneously without forcing anyone to compromise.**

**Most Noted serves everyone**
The Most Noted mechanic isn't just for Vibe Players — it's a second recognition track that can surface a grief-round submission, an iconoclast's deliberately left-field pick, or a Wise Elder's deep cut that moved people to write but didn't win votes. It rewards resonance over consensus.

**Crowd-sourced round themes are a feature**
The Organizer's instinct to crowd-source themes reflects a value the whole product should embody: this is a shared experience, not a platform you play on. Round theme suggestion and voting should be a first-class feature.

**The Outsider is who we're building for**
They already left. Every design decision should be run through the question: would this have kept them in the room?
